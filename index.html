Philosophy

UX & DESIGN TENETS

01

IT'S A DESIGN DEMOCRACY. TRUST YOUR ELECTED OFFICIALS.

Good ideas can come from anywhere. UX teams should champion great ideas regardless of source, while trusting their own capabilities to uncover true user needs.

02

TOP-DOWN MEETS BOTTOMS-UP

UX teams only succeed when creating compelling vision (top-down) while breaking things into executable pieces (bottoms-up). You have to execute on both fronts simultaneously.

03

BE THE PERSON WHO MAKES THINGS SIMPLER

Making things simple is the hardest and most valuable skill. Up-level, summarize, rephrase, talk about actions to take. Don't dazzle with complexity.

04

FOCUS ON USERS AND SCENARIOS, NOT FEATURES

What job did customers hire your product for? Don't start with features—start with what users really want to get out of your product.

05

IF USERS CAN'T FIND IT, IT DOESN'T EXIST

At Microsoft, the top 10 feature requests were features that already existed. Discovery and first experience are key to successful products.

06

INTERNALIZE PROCESS TO KNOW WHAT TO SKIP

Know your design process well enough to understand which steps aren't always necessary. Great UX professionals know what they can skip and when.

07

IF NO ONE COMPLAINS, THE BAR ISN'T HIGH ENOUGH

If Engineering consistently says your UX team is easy to work with, compromises are likely being made too quickly in favor of implementation over user experience.

08

DEEP DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE IS REQUIRED

Designers need technical training, sales exposure, infrastructure understanding, and competitive landscape knowledge to have significant impact beyond surface-level improvements.

09

IT'S ALL ABOUT USER PERCEPTION

User perception is the only effective way to measure UX success. A skyscraper fixed elevator complaints by installing lobby mirrors—perception, not performance.

10

CONVINCE A BUYER TO BUY, A USER TO RENEW

Target buyer personas to get customers on board. But if you want renewals, you need to convince the actual users that it's worth it.

11

ALL GOOD THINGS START WITH PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY

The ability for anyone to feel safe to speak their mind is crucial to any well-functioning team. A lack of it, even from a single team member, can destroy performance.

12

BE CLEAR, NOT CLEVER

Being clear when working with teams and clear in product experience always trumps being clever. Clarity delivers great products and teams that collaborate well over time.
